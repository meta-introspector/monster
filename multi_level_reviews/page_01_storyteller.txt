# Storyteller Inspiration - Page 01

**Focus**: Narrative, accessibility, engagement

 The image shows a document titled "MONSTER GROUP: A LEADERSHIP AND TEAMWORK PROGRAM FOR PEOPLE WITH AUTISM", which outlines a program for helping individuals with autism develop leadership and teamwork skills. To inspire others, the narrative could be explained as follows:

The Monster Group program is a powerful metaphor for how people with autism can harness their unique strengths to become leaders in their own right and contribute to a team environment. Just like how the "monsters" on this page are depicted, individuals with autism may have certain traits that can be perceived as "scary" or different from those of non-autistic individuals. However, these traits—often referred to as "superpowers"—can be leveraged when given the right tools and guidance.

The compelling narrative would emphasize that while these individuals might initially feel like outsiders ("monsters"), they can learn to navigate the challenges of social interaction through structured programs like the Monster Group. These programs aim to help them develop essential skills such as communication, problem-solving, and empathy, which are crucial for leadership roles in any team setting.

The document outlines the program's structure, detailing that it consists of three groups: a 12-week course on leadership and teamwork, a mentorship program, and ongoing support through access to the company's facilities and resources. The metaphors used here can be interpreted as inviting those with autism into an inclusive environment where they will not only learn but also find a sense of belonging within the "team".

In essence, the narrative would highlight that just as the monsters in the image are not inherently evil or dangerous, individuals with autism have valuable contributions to offer. They can be transformative leaders and team members when given the right opportunities for growth and development, which is what the Monster Group program aims to provide. The program acts as a bridge between the perceived "monstrosity" of autism and the potential for these individuals to thrive in inclusive environments.
